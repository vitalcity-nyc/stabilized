The math behind the next Rent Guidelines Board vote

Before the June 2026 vote · Order #58

Costs rose 5.3%. The formula yields 4.5%. The mayor wants 0%. Here is the math the board will weigh.

In late June, the Rent Guidelines Board will set the rent increase for roughly a million rent-stabilized apartments for the lease year starting October 1, 2026. It will be the first such vote under Mayor Mamdani, who appointed six new members in February and ran on a four-year rent freeze. The board's annual cost index, published April 9, says landlord costs rose 5.3%. The numbers the staff's formulas produce are inputs to the board's decision, not recommendations — by statute the vote is a qualitative judgment. Below: the math the board has in front of it, three other defensible ways to read the same data, and what each implies.

Why three formulas? Each one treats landlord profit differently. Net revenue holds the dollar amount of profit flat year to year. Inflation-adjusted lets profit grow with inflation, so its real value doesn't erode. Traditional is the original 1969 formula, simpler than the other two, and looks at next year's expected costs as well as this year's. The board may consider any of them — but the staff's report is explicit that they're illustrative measures, not recommendations: the vote weighs many factors, including public testimony and the broader housing picture. 2026 cost index, pp. 11–12

The averages hide the story

Two stocks, not one. The same vote affects them very differently.

"Rent-stabilized building" lumps together a recently built mostly market-rate tower in core Manhattan with a 100% stabilized prewar walk-up in the Bronx. Rent Guidelines Board decisions land identically on both. Their finances do not. Howard Slatkin and Alex Armlovich have shown that the average masks the bifurcation: one group is fine, the other is sliding toward distress.

Rent-stabilized buildings are doing well financially, as long as you're talking about buildings that mostly aren't rent-stabilized. Howard Slatkin · Vital City

One number, two stocks · what the formulas can't see

Because the board votes one number per lease type for the whole city, every formula on this site — official or alternate — applies the same increase to a fully rent-stabilized prewar walk-up in the Bronx and a mostly market-rate 421-a tower in Hudson Yards. Armlovich proposes a fix the board could adopt on its own: tier the guideline by building condition. Use the existing city enforcement list of the worst-maintained buildings (the worst 3–5% of regulated stock) to deny increases to chronic-violator landlords while letting well-run buildings receive cost-justified adjustments. That structural change is outside this calculator's scope, but the bifurcation above is the case for it.

Same law, different yardsticks

Other defensible ways to do the math.

The law names four things the board has to consider — taxes, operating costs, financing, the supply of housing — plus the cost of living. The staff's formula handles those one specific way: hold landlord profit flat as costs go up. There are other reasonable yardsticks. Historical precedent · the 2015–16 freezes

The board has voted a 0% guideline on 1-year leases twice — under Order #47 (2015–16) and Order #48 (2016–17). Per Armlovich, those freezes were aligned with the data: the 2016 cost index showed operating costs fell 1.2% that year, primarily energy. A 2026 freeze would happen under cost growth of +5.3%, an entirely different macro condition. Armlovich · Vital City

Limits of this model. It applies to the rent-stabilized stock as a single average. Sources, math, assumptions

Methodology — show your work.

This dashboard reproduces the New York City Rent Guidelines Board's own published formulas using the staff's own published inputs, then adds alternative formulas grounded in the same statutory criteria, and surfaces independent analyses of the bifurcated stock and the financial distress threshold. Every number has a source link. No calculation is hidden.

The legal frame

The board operates under New York's Rent Stabilization Law of 1969, NYC Administrative Code §26-510 (full text also at Title 26, Chapter 4). Subsection (b) directs the board to consider real estate taxes; operating and maintenance costs (insurance, governmental fees, fuel, labor); the cost and availability of financing; the overall supply of housing and overall vacancy rates; the cost of living; and "such other data as may be made available."

The official three formulas

From the 2026 cost index report (pp. 11–12, endnotes 8–11):

  1. Net revenue (profit held flat) · required rent change = (operating-cost share of rent) × (cost increase). The result is split into a 1-year and 2-year guideline using the share of leases on each term, so that on average the rent rolling in goes up by exactly the required amount. Profit (rent minus costs) stays at the same dollar amount.
  2. Inflation-adjusted profit · same as net revenue, then add (profit share of rent) × (inflation, excluding rent) so profit doesn't lose purchasing power. From 2026 onward, the board uses inflation-excluding-rent rather than headline inflation, so rent isn't chasing rent.
  3. Traditional · the original 1969 formula. Covers cost growth only, not profit. 1-year output uses this year's cost change; 2-year uses the average of this year's and next year's projected cost change. Doesn't consider the mix of 1-year and 2-year leases.

The alternates

  • Pure cost pass-through · no profit-cushion adjustment; pass the cost increase through directly, splitting between 1-year and 2-year by lease mix.
  • Small-building weighted · approximation that uplifts the cost figure to reflect heavier insurance and labor share in buildings with 19 or fewer units.
  • Add a capital reserve · adds an annual capital-replacement reserve (default 1.5% of rent) to the cost basket, since the official cost index leaves capital expenses out.
  • Tie to NYC wage growth · increase set to the federal wage data for NYC private-sector workers.
  • Tie to renter income growth · increase set to median renter household income growth from Census American Community Survey data.
  • Hold rent burden steady · increase that keeps median rent-to-income unchanged.
  • Tie to inflation, excluding rent · federal inflation index for the NYC area, with the rent component stripped out.
  • Ceiling and floor · the official formula, capped above by NYC wage growth and floored below at zero.
  • Affordability cap · the official formula, capped at renter income growth.

The freeze module

For each modeled freeze year, rent collected is held flat, operating costs grow at the user-supplied rate (default = the 2026 cost index), and profit is recomputed. The mortgage cushion is approximated from the leverage and starting cushion the user supplies. Tenant savings are aggregate dollars not paid versus the rent path the chosen formula would have produced. The "100,000 units at risk" figure is Armlovich's, derived from the income and expense study's ~10% distressed share applied to the regulated stock.

What this dashboard does not do

  • It does not estimate unit-level rent. State-registered rents are confidential.
  • It does not model effects on new construction. The rent-stabilized stock is overwhelmingly pre-1974 plus tax-benefit buildings.
  • It does not model legal pathways for deregulation, capital improvement increases, or preferential rents — those operate alongside the guideline and changed materially with the 2019 rent law.
  • It applies one citywide guideline; per the bifurcation tab, that is the board's actual posture, but a single average hides the tails of the distribution.
