Before the June 2026 vote · Order #58

Costs rose 5.3 percent. The formula recommends 4.5. The mayor wants 0. Here is the math the board will face.

In late June, the Rent Guidelines Board will set the rent increase for roughly one million stabilized apartments for the lease year beginning October 1, 2026. It will be the first such vote under Mayor Mamdani, who appointed six new members in February and ran on a four-year rent freeze. The 2026 Price Index of Operating Costs, published April 9, says costs rose 5.3 percent. Below: the formula the staff actually uses, three other ways to read it, and what the math implies.

The averages hide the story

Two stocks, not one. The same vote affects them very differently.

"Rent-stabilized building" lumps together a recently built mostly market-rate tower in core Manhattan with a 100-percent stabilized prewar walk-up in the Bronx. RGB decisions land identically on both. Their finances do not. Howard Slatkin and Alex Armlovich have shown that the average masks the bifurcation: one group is fine, the other is sliding toward distress.

Rent-stabilized buildings are doing well financially, as long as you're talking about buildings that mostly aren't rent-stabilized. Howard Slatkin · Vital City

Differential guidelines · what the formulas can't see

Because the RGB votes one number per lease type for the whole city, every formula on this site — official or alternate — applies the same increase to a fully-stabilized prewar walk-up in the Bronx and a mostly-market 421-a tower in Hudson Yards. Armlovich proposes a fix the board can adopt by rulemaking: tier the guideline by building condition, using the existing Alternative Enforcement Program (AEP) threshold (worst 3–5% of regulated stock) to deny increases to chronic-violator buildings while letting compliant buildings receive cost-justified adjustments. That kind of structural change is outside this calculator's scope, but the bifurcation analysis above is the case for it.

Historical precedent · the 2015–16 freezes

The board has voted a 0% guideline on 1-year leases twice — under Order #47 (2015–16) and Order #48 (2016–17). Per Armlovich, those freezes were aligned with the data: the 2016 PIOC showed operating costs fell 1.2 percent that year, primarily energy. A 2026 freeze would happen under cost growth of +5.3 percent, an entirely different macro condition. Armlovich · Vital City

Sources, math, assumptions

Methodology — show your work.

This dashboard reproduces the New York City Rent Guidelines Board's own published formulas using the staff's own published inputs, then adds alternative formulas grounded in the same statutory criteria, and surfaces independent analyses of the bifurcated stock and the financial distress threshold. Every number has a source link. No calculation is hidden.

The statutory frame

The board operates under Rent Stabilization Law §26-510 (also at NYC Admin Code Title 26 Ch. 4). Subdivision (b) directs the board to consider real estate taxes; gross operating and maintenance costs (insurance, governmental fees, fuel, labor); cost and availability of financing; the overall supply of housing and overall vacancy rates; CPI; and "such other data as may be made available."

The official three formulas

From the 2026 PIOC report (pp. 11–12, endnotes 8–11):

  1. Net revenue · required revenue change = O&M-share-of-revenue × PIOC%. Splits into a 1-year and 2-year guideline using the lease mix from HCR registrations such that (L1 × R1) + (L2 × R2 / 2) = required revenue change. NOI is held nominally constant.
  2. CPI-adjusted NOI · same as net revenue, then add (NOI-share × CPI-less-shelter) to compensate for inflation eroding NOI. From 2026 onward, CPI-less-shelter is used (rather than full CPI) so rent isn't chasing rent.
  3. Traditional · the original 1969 formula. Adjusts only owner expenses, not NOI. 1-year output uses the current PIOC; 2-year uses the average of current PIOC and the staff's PIOC projection for next year. Ignores the lease mix.

The alternates

  • Pure PIOC pass-through · no NOI translation; pass through PIOC% directly, splitting 1/2-year by lease mix.
  • Small-building weighted · proxy that re-weights toward heavier insurance and labor exposure of buildings with 19 or fewer units.
  • Capital-inclusive · adds an annual capital-replacement reserve (default 1.5% of revenue) to the operating-cost basket.
  • Wage-indexed · BLS QCEW NYC private-sector average wage growth.
  • Renter-income-indexed · ACS median renter household income growth.
  • Rent-burden-stable · the increase that keeps median rent-to-income constant.
  • Renter CPI ex-shelter · BLS CPI all-items-less-shelter for the NY area.
  • Min/max guardrails · official commensurate, capped above by wage growth and floored below at zero.
  • Affordability cap · official commensurate, capped at renter income growth.

The bifurcation analysis

The "Two stocks, not one" tab summarizes Howard Slatkin's 2025 essay for Vital City and Alex Armlovich's roadmap. Both draw on RGB Income & Expense Study micro-data, in particular the per-borough revenue/cost panels and Table 8 on the share of buildings with operating costs at or above rental income. The percentages are reproduced as published; their underlying year is 2022–2023 for the revenue/cost growth panel, 2025 for the I&E Table 8 distress share.

The freeze module

For each modeled freeze year, revenue is held flat, operating costs grow at the user-supplied rate (default = current PIOC), and NOI is recomputed. DSCR is approximated from leverage. Tenant savings are aggregate dollars not paid versus the counterfactual rent path that the chosen formula would have produced. The "100,000 units at risk" figure is Armlovich's, derived from RGB Table 8's ~10% distressed share applied to the regulated stock.

What this dashboard does not do

  • It does not estimate unit-level rent. DHCR registered rents are confidential.
  • It does not model effects on new construction. The stabilized stock is overwhelmingly pre-1974 plus tax-benefit buildings.
  • It does not model post-HSTPA legal pathways for deregulation, capital-improvement (MCI / IAI) increases, or preferential rent dynamics.
  • It applies one citywide guideline; per the bifurcation tab, that is the board's actual posture, but the math hides the tail of the distribution.
