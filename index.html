By statute, by formula

Costs rose 5.3 percent. The formula recommends 4.5. The board voted 3.0. Here is the math.

The New York City Rent Guidelines Board sets the annual rent increase for roughly one million rent-stabilized apartments. The Rent Stabilization Law tells the board what to consider; the board's research staff translates that into a recommended number through a procedure called the commensurate rent adjustment. There are three current variants — and the board's vote rarely matches any of them.

Historical precedent: the 2015–16 freezes

The board has voted a 0% guideline on 1-year leases twice — under Order #47 (lease year 2015–16) and Order #48 (2016–17), both with the 2-year lease still set at 2%. These remain the only freezes in the board's history; RGB Income & Expense Studies covering those years are the natural-experiment evidence.

Limits of this model. It covers the rent-stabilized stock only. Effects on new construction are minimal because post-1974 / non-tax-benefit buildings aren't in this system. After HSTPA 2019 closed most exit ramps (high-rent decontrol, vacancy bonuses), conversions and warehousing dynamics changed materially. Capital improvement and individual apartment improvement increases continue under a guideline freeze. HSTPA 2019

Sources, math, assumptions

Methodology — show your work.

This dashboard reproduces the New York City Rent Guidelines Board's own published formulas using the staff's own published inputs, then adds a set of alternative formulas grounded in the same statutory criteria. Every number has a source link. No calculation is hidden.

The statutory frame

The board operates under Rent Stabilization Law §26-510 (also at NYC Admin Code Title 26 Ch. 4). Subdivision (b) directs the board to consider real estate taxes; gross operating and maintenance costs (insurance, governmental fees, fuel, labor); the cost and availability of financing; the overall supply of housing and overall vacancy rates; CPI; and "such other data as may be made available." The dashboard maps each of these to the RGB report that measures it.

The official three formulas

From the 2026 PIOC report (pp. 11–12, endnotes 8–11):

  1. Net revenue · required revenue change = O&M-share-of-revenue × PIOC%. Splits into a 1-year and 2-year guideline using the lease mix from HCR registrations such that (L1 × R1) + (L2 × R2 / 2) = required revenue change. NOI is held nominally constant.
  2. CPI-adjusted NOI · same as net revenue, then add (NOI-share × CPI-less-shelter) to compensate for inflation eroding NOI. From 2026 onward, CPI-less-shelter is used (rather than full CPI) so rent isn't chasing rent.
  3. Traditional · the original 1969 formula. Adjusts only owner expenses, not NOI. 1-year output uses the current PIOC; 2-year uses the average of current PIOC and the staff's PIOC projection for next year. Ignores the lease mix.

The alternates

All alternates use the same statutory frame and the same year's inputs.

  • Pure PIOC pass-through · no NOI translation; pass through PIOC% directly, splitting 1/2-year by lease mix.
  • Small-building weighted · proxy that re-weights the PIOC components toward heavier insurance and labor exposure of buildings with 19 or fewer units. Implemented here by scaling the headline PIOC up by a small-building multiplier; replace with re-weighted PIOC components when the next I&E study publishes them.
  • Capital-inclusive · adds an annual capital-replacement reserve component (default 1.5% of revenue) to the operating-cost basket before applying the net-revenue formula.
  • Wage-indexed · increase set equal to the latest BLS QCEW NYC private-sector average wage growth, splitting into 1/2-year leases at the commensurate ratio (2-year ≈ 1-year × commensurate-ratio).
  • Renter-income-indexed · increase set equal to ACS median renter household income growth.
  • Rent-burden-stable · solves for the increase that keeps median rent-to-income constant given the renter-income input.
  • Renter CPI ex-shelter · increase set equal to BLS CPI all-items-less-shelter for the NY area.
  • Min/max guardrails · official commensurate, capped above by QCEW wage growth and floored below by zero.
  • Affordability cap · the official commensurate, capped at the lower of (a) commensurate or (b) renter-income growth.

The freeze module

For each modeled freeze year, revenue is held flat, operating costs grow at the user-supplied rate (default = current PIOC), and NOI is recomputed. DSCR is approximated from leverage: starting NOI per dollar of revenue × (1 / (LTV × cap-rate-implied debt service)) — the user's starting DSCR is rebased against the trajectory. Tenant savings are aggregate dollars not paid versus the counterfactual rent path that the chosen formula would have produced. The historical precedent panel cites Orders #47 and #48 (1-year freezes in 2015–16 and 2016–17).

What this dashboard does not do

  • It does not estimate unit-level rent. DHCR registered rents are confidential; only building-level unit counts are publicly available (via DOF tax bills, scraped by taxbills.nyc).
  • It does not model effects on new construction. The stabilized stock is overwhelmingly pre-1974 plus tax-benefit buildings; new construction sits outside the system except via 421-a / 485-x.
  • It does not model post-HSTPA legal pathways for deregulation, capital improvement increases (MCI / IAI), or preferential rent dynamics — those are separate from the guideline.
